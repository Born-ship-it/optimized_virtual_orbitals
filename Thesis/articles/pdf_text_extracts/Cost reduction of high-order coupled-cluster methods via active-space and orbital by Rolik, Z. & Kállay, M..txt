Cost reduction of high-order coupled-cluster methods via active-space and

Zoltán Rolik and Mihály Kállaya)

We discuss several techniques which have the potential to decrease the computational expenses of
high-order coupled-cluster (CC) methods with a reasonable loss in accuracy. In particular, the CC
singles, doubles, and triples (CCSDT) as well as the CC singles, doubles, triples, and perturbativequadruples [CCSDT(Q)] methods are considered, which are frequently used in high-precision model
chemistries for the calculation of iterative triples and quadruples corrections. First, we study the pos-
sibilities for using active spaces to decrease the computational costs. In this case, an active space is
deﬁned and some indices of cluster amplitudes are restricted to be in the space. Second, the applica-
tion of transformed virtual orbitals is investigated. In this framework, to reduce the computation timethe dimension of the properly transformed virtual one-particle space is truncated. We have found
that the orbital transformation techniques outperform the active-space approaches. Using the trans-
formation techniques, the computational time can be reduced in average by an order of magnitudewithout signiﬁcant loss in accuracy. It is demonstrated that high-order CC calculations are possible
for considerably larger systems than before using the implemented techniques. © 2011 American
Institute of Physics . [doi: 10.1063/1.3569829 ]
I. INTRODUCTION
In the last decade, computational quantum chemistry has
developed to a stage where it is capable of providing molec-
ular properties with quantitative accuracy. The existence of
efﬁcient electronic-structure methods and analytic deriva-tive techniques, convergent basis-set hierarchies and basis-
set extrapolation schemes, relativistic ab initio approaches,
and post-Born–Oppenheimer corrections inspired the devel-opment of high-accuracy model chemistries
1–9which enabled
calculations with chemical ( ∼1 kcal/mol) or with subchemi-
cal (∼1 kJ/mol) accuracy for small molecules. The common
feature of these composite schemes is that they are based
on the coupled-cluster (CC) hierarchy of methods. In par-
ticular, the low-level correlation effects are taken into ac-
count via the CC singles, doubles, and perturbative triples
[CCSD(T)] approach,10while the higher-order correlation
contributions are evaluated using the CC singles, doubles, and
triples (CCSDT) (Ref. 11) as well as the CC singles, dou-
bles, triples, and perturbative quadruples [CCSDT(Q)]12–14
or the CC singles, doubles, triples, and quadruples (CCS-
DTQ) (Refs. 15–17) methods. Unfortunately, the scaling of
the latter approaches with the size of the systems is steep,which seriously hinders the extension of high-precision model
chemistries toward larger molecules. For this reason, it is
tempting to pursue strategies to decrease the computationalexpenses of high-order CC methods.
There have been several attempts to develop cost-efﬁcient
approximations to CC methods. The efforts along this linecan be classiﬁed in three categories: the active-space (AS) CC
a)Electronic mail: kallay@mail.bme.hu.approaches, the methods based on the transformation and sub-
sequent truncation of the one-electron basis set, and the ap-
proximations using some kind of decomposition of the wavefunction parameters or integral lists.
The AS approach was originally published by Oliphant
and Adamowicz
15and was intended to include the static elec-
tron correlation into the CC theory using the single-reference
(SR) formalism. These authors supposed that there is anotherimportant determinant in addition to the Fermi vacuum, which
can be generated by double substitution from the latter. The
two determinants together constitute the AS. Single and dou-ble excitations out of the secondary determinant can be sim-
ulated by higher excitations with respect to the Fermi vac-
uum with partially ﬁxed indices. The ﬁxed indices correspondto those two orbitals in which the two determinants differ.
This formally multireference CC method was later general-
ized by Piecuch and co-workers
18,19to more than two or-
bitals, and implemented for arbitrary ASs by Kállay et al.20
Later Köhn and Olsen21developed a generalization of the
AS CC approach using a more sophisticated classiﬁcation ofthe virtual orbitals and presented promising CCSD, CCSDT,
and CCSDTQ results for SR systems. The AS approach have
also been implemented by Fan and Hirata
22up to the quadru-
ples level of theory and was applied to describe the singlet-
triplet separation for triatomic hydrides. The philosophy ofthe AS approach has been utilized in the framework of the
equation of motion CC theory by Kowalski and Piecuch,
23,24
and Kowalski et al.25These methods were applied to describe
the electronic state of the Be 3cluster25and excited, ionized,
and electron-attached states of small molecules.26
Though the AS approach was originally designed for sys-
tems with pronounced multireference character, it can also be

In the second category of cost-reduction techniques the
set of virtual orbitals is divided into active and inactive sub-
spaces, and the inactive orbitals are dropped. Prior to that,utilizing the rotational invariance of SR CC methods, a trans-
formation of the virtual orbitals is performed to minimize the
error coming from the neglect of inactive virtual orbitals in
the subsequent correlated calculations.
The separation of the important part of the virtual space
is frequently performed by the application of natural orbitals
(NOs).
27–29The eigenvalues of the one-particle density matrix
obtained from a wave function ansatz can be considered as thepopulation of the corresponding orbitals, i.e., the NOs. Using
this simple physical interpretation the removal of the weakly
populated NOs leads to a simple method to reduce the one-particle basis set. A plausible choice for the construction of
the truncated virtual space for the purpose of CC calculations
is to use the NOs obtained with the ﬁrst-order Møller–Plesset(MP) wave function.
30–33
In several approaches, a functional of the orbital rota-
tion parameters in the virtual space is deﬁned to maximize(or minimize) the value of some physical quantity. The corre-
sponding modiﬁed orbitals are often referred to as optimized
virtual orbitals (OVOs). Adamowicz et al.
34,35minimized a
modiﬁed second-order Hylleraas-functional with respect to
the parameters of the rotation among the active and inac-tive orbitals. Later Sosa et al.
32showed that the Hylleraas-
functional-based approach gives more accurate results for
the CCSD[T] energy36than the simple truncation based on
MP2 NOs. For the applications where the OVOs serve the
cost-reduction of high-order CC calculations, the approach of
Neogrády, Pito ˇnák, and Urban37is more attractive. In their
study two functionals were proposed, one of them is based on
the CC energy expression, while the other makes use of the
overlap of the CC wave functions deﬁned over the full and theactive virtual spaces, and it was demonstrated in several test
calculations that the overlap functional gives more accurate
results for the CCSD and CCSD(T) energies than the energyfunctional.
We note that the error introduced by the above approxi-
mations can be signiﬁcantly reduced via a simple correctionoriginally suggested by Adamowicz and Bartlett,
34who eval-
uated the CCSD(T) energy as a sum of the MP2 energy calcu-
lated with the full basis set and the difference of the CCSD(T)
and MP2 energies obtained with the truncated virtual space.
It was demonstrated in their study and later by Taube andBartlett
38and Pito ˇnaket al.39that the correction signiﬁcantly
improves the performance of the methods due to error com-
pensation.
We should also mention the so-called multiple basis set
approach of Klopper and co-workers,40which can be ap-
plied with any of the virtual orbital optimization methods dis-cussed above to accelerate the calculations of the perturbative
triples correction in the CCSD(T) method. In this approach,the CCSD equations are solved with the original basis set,
then the singles and doubles amplitudes are projected into the
reduced space, where the triples correction is calculated.
The methods using different decomposition techniques
are out of the scope of the present study; thus, the corre-
sponding approaches are only brieﬂy mentioned here. Reviv-ing an old idea of Beebe and Linderberg,
41Koch et al. used
the Cholesky decomposition to factorize the two-electron in-
tegral list and signiﬁcantly decreased the computational ex-penses of ab initio methods including CC approaches.
42,43
Koch and Sánchez de Merás44also investigated the Cholesky-
decomposition of the CCSD(T) energy denominators which
allows the factorization of CCSD(T) energy diagrams to ob-
tain a reduced-scaling algorithm. Finally, Kinoshita, Hino,and Bartlett
45,46applied the singular value decomposition
(SVD) to the matrix of cluster amplitudes to develop cost-
effective iterative CC methods.
In this account, we examine if the above-mentioned
scaling-reduction techniques based on the AS approxima-
tions or the truncation of the one-electron basis can be ap-plied to high-order CC methods. Several numerical stud-
ies have been already published about the efﬁciency of
these approaches for the calculation of CCSD and CCSD(T)energies;
21,32,37,39,47,48thus, the latter are not considered here.

In Sec. III, the results of our benchmark calculations are

the various approaches. To conﬁrm the raison d’etre of the
computation time reduction techniques large-scale CCSDTand CCSDT(Q) calculations are also presented for bigger

II. THEORY
A. Error of the approximations
The principal aim of this paper is to develop reduced-cost
CCSDT and CCSDT(Q) methods using various computationtime reduction techniques, which enable the cheaper evalua-
tion of the iterative triples and perturbative quadruples contri-
butions required in high-accuracy model chemistries. That is,
we intend to compute

it is hardly possible to keep this error constant. However, in
most of the chemical processes only a small part of a large

C. MP2 frozen natural orbitals
Diagonalizing the virtual block of a one-particle density
matrix and retaining only the virtual orbitals with high popu-
lation result in a reduced-size basis set called the frozen nat-ural orbital (FNO) basis set, which hopefully contains the
essential part of the virtual space. Since the computational
cost of the CCSDT and CCSDT(Q) methods scales as theﬁfth power of n
v, the reduction of the virtual space implies
a speedup which is proportional to ( nv/nav)5.
In the CC case, the simplest approximation of the wave
function is the ﬁrst-order MP wave function. In our study, the
virtual part of the density matrix constructed from the ﬁrst-
order MP wave function was diagonalized to obtain the virtualNOs, and the NOs with low population were neglected. Since
the working equations for perturbative CC approaches such
as CCSD(T) and CCSDT(Q) were derived for a one-electron
basis where the Fock-matrix is diagonal, we constructed the
canonical orbitals in the reduced one-particle space. This ap-proach will be referred to as MP2 FNO.
32
To ensure the error cancellation, we used the same re-
duced MP2 FNO basis set for the calculation of both terms atthe right hand side of Eqs. (1)and(2); thus, no additional cor-
rections are needed as in the case of the CCSD and CCSD(T)
methods.
34,39
For the truncation of the virtual NO space there exist sev-
eral schemes. In the simplest case, one deﬁnes an appropriate
threshold for the NO occupation numbers and drops the NOsbelow this threshold. Another criterion has recently been in-
troduced by Landau et al. ,
33which was also adopted in our
work. In this scheme, for each NO the occupation numberof that particular NO and all the NOs with larger occupation
numbers are summed up. This cumulative occupation num-
ber is divided by the number of electrons; thus, the result-
ing number—hereafter referred to as ε—lies between 0 and 1.
Only those virtual orbitals are retained for which εis smaller
then a threshold, e.g., 0.9. This algorithm results in relative
errors for various properties which are less sensitive to the
properties of the studied systems and the employed basis sets.

III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
A. Computational details
To assess the performance of the AS CC, MP2 FNO,
and OVOs techniques, numerous test calculations were car-ried out for small and medium-sized molecules. The results
are also compared to the efﬁciency of the simple elimination
of canonical HF MOs on the basis of orbital energies. All cal-
culations were performed using the
MRCC suite of quantum
chemistry programs.51In the AS CC calculations, we usedthe implementation of the AS CC approach as described in
Ref. 20, while for the MP2 FNO and OVOs calculations addi-
tional modules have been developed. In the case of MP2 FNO,the approximate MP2 density matrix has been coded, and
a four-index integral transformation routine has been writ-
ten. Connected to the OVOs method, we have also imple-mented the self-consistent iteration process discussed in the
Appendix. Canonical HF MO integrals were taken from the
CFOUR program package.52
As we pointed out in Sec. II, our aim was to monitor
the performance of the cost-reduction techniques for the it-
erative triples and quadruples CC contributions for the pur-
pose of high-accuracy calculations. Since these contributions
are usually evaluated with Dunning’s correlation consistentpolarized triple- (cc-pVTZ) and double- ζ(cc-pVDZ) basis
sets,
53respectively, in high-precision model chemistries,1–9
we also chose these basis sets. In our calculations, the core
electrons were kept frozen, and the geometries optimized at
the CCSD(T) level of theory with the cc-pVQZ basis set were
used. As we discussed in Sec. II, to ensure the required accu-
racy the results with relative errors of less than 50 and 15% are
considered acceptable for the iterative triples and quadruples
corrections, respectively.

First, we carried out a systematic study for the 30 atoms
and molecules of the HEAT test set.4We have found that
our cost-reduction approaches are efﬁcient for molecules con-
taining at least two heavy atoms and one hydrogen atom,
but for smaller molecules, due to the small number of basisfunctions and electrons, they only give reliable results if the
truncation of the virtual space is not signiﬁcant. Here, only
two representative examples are selected, the carbon-dioxideand hydrogen-peroxide molecules, to present the behavior of
the approaches. The selection of these molecules is justiﬁed
because the contribution of dynamic correlation, and conse-quently that of triple and quadruple excitations to the total
energies, is signiﬁcant. Furthermore, CO

calculations
13,54and were considered as the upper limit for

E. Timings
In accordance with the above results about 50 and 60%
of the virtual orbitals can be dropped at the CCSDT andCCSDT(Q) calculations for butadiene and benzene, respec-
tively, using the orbital transformation approaches, and thus,
in principle, speedups of 32 and about 100 can be achieved.One may be interested in the effective speedups which can be
measured using our implementation. Therefore we compiled
computation times in Table IVfor the butadiene and benzene
molecules, which are large enough for such analysis. The cal-
culations were carried out on machines with Intel Xeon E3110
3.00 GHz CPUs and 8 Gb main memory.
The effective speedups in the smaller basis set fall be-
hind the theoretical ones especially for the CCSDT method.
In contrast, the measured speedup for the CCSDT methods
with the cc-pVTZ basis set is very close to the theoretical one
for butadiene and much larger than the theoretical value forbenzene. The latter unexpected speedup is a consequence of
the considerably larger memory requirement of the untrun-
cated calculation. The size of the cluster amplitude matrices,integral lists, and intermediates scales as the third or fourth
power of the virtual-space size in the case of a CCSDT cal-
culation, thus only a smaller portion of the elements of thesearrays can ﬁt into the memory at the same time for the untrun-
cated calculation, which results in increased I/O demand. We
think that due to the steep memory requirement of the calcu-lations the ratio of the measured and theoretical speedups is
even higher for bigger systems.

IV. CONCLUDING REMARKS
We have studied the AS CC, MP2 FNO, and OVOs cost-
reduction techniques for the approximation of iterative triples
as well as quadruples contributions for the purpose of high-
precision calculations. It has been shown that the AS CC ap-proach can be used to reduce the computational price, but
the gain in the speed is signiﬁcantly lower than that for the
MP2 FNO or OVOs methods. No remarkable difference hasbeen observed between the performance of the MP2 FNO and
OVOs methods, though the simplicity of the MP2 FNO ap-
proach with respect to the latter makes it more favorable.
Our MP2 FNO test calculations demonstrate that the use
of the ε=0.95 threshold for the cumulative population of
the retained virtual orbitals is enough to obtain the quadru-ples contribution with a reasonable loss in accuracy thereby
reducing the computational time by about an order of mag-
nitude. For the approximation of the triples contribution alarger εvalue, e.g., 0.975, might be needed. Despite this the
achievable speedup, a factor of several tens, can be larger than
that for the quadruples since these calculations are performed
with a larger, triple- ζbasis set. With even larger basis sets the
speedup can be signiﬁcantly better. We have also shown thatthe errors of the heats of formation calculated by the reduced-
cost CC methods are in the same range as the errors of the
individual molecules. The calculations for naphthalene andanthracene demonstrate that the MP2 FNO method allows for
the calculation of the iterative triples and quadruples contribu-
tions for molecules of more than 40 electrons on commoditycomputers.
We note that we also explored several further ways for the
reduction of the costs of high-order CC methods including theSVD of the cluster amplitude matrices for both iterative and
perturbative CC models, the Cholesky decomposition of the
energy denominators in the case of CCSDT(Q), or the factor-ization of the two-electron integral list. Though a moderate
reduction in the computational expenses can also be achieved
using these techniques, they cannot compete with the orbital
transformation approaches discussed here. In a subsequent
paper, we will show that the combination of the MP2 FNOscheme with localized approaches results in an even more ro-
bust cost-reduction technique for high-order CC methods and
allows for high-accuracy calculations for signiﬁcantly largermolecules.
