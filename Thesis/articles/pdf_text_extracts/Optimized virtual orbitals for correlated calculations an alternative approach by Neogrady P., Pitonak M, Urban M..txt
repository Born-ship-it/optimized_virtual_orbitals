We propose an alternative formulation for creating the optimized virtual orbital space
(OVOS). Our technique exploits and extends the method developed by Adamowicz andco-workers [L. Adamowicz, R.J. Bartlett. J. chem. Phys. ,86, 6314 (1987); L. Adamowicz,
R.J. Bartlett, A.J. Sadlej. J. chem. Phys. ,88, 5749 (1988).]. The aim of the OVOS technique is
to reduce the original SCF basis of the virtual molecular orbitals and to reduce the computer
time in the coupled cluster (CC) and related highly sophisticated correlated methods. OVOSis created by using an invariant unitary rotation of the virtual orbitals subspace.
New optimization functionals are proposed and implemented. The ﬁrst type are ‘energy’
functionals. Their optimization leads to the minimal difference between the CCSD, CCD, orthe second-order perturbation energy, MP2, in the original orbital basis and the OVOS basis,respectively. Alternatively, linearized ‘overlap’ functionals optimize the overlap between
the correlated wave function in the full and the OVOS space, respectively. The original
exponential parametrization was replaced by the efﬁcient algorithm for the virtual orbitalsubspace rotation based on the Lagrangian multipliers technique which ensures orthogonalitywithin rotated virtual orbitals.
The method is illustrated by calculations of correlation energies and/or reaction energies,
spectroscopic constants or dipole moments of HF, HCN, HNC, CO and F
2molecules and
dissociation energies of pentane to propene and ethane. The ‘overlap’ functional is shown tobe more efﬁcient than the ‘energy’ one, particularly in representing triple excitations using
OVOS. The basis set dependence of the efﬁciency of the OVOS technique was also studied.

2.2. Partitioning of the virtual orbital space and
notation used
The partitioning of the virtual orbital space (see ﬁgure 1)
is suggested with the prospect of calculations of bothclosed shell as well as high-spin open shell systems:
(1)V1-subspace—inactive virtual orbitals : Virtual
orbitals, which remain unchanged in the optimiza-tion procedure. This subspace is important partic-ularly in open shell calculations, where at least allhalf-occupied orbitals are contained in V1. This
guarantees no O–V mixing, since orbitals, which areoccupied by /C11electrons are virtual for electrons
with /C12spin. The V1 subspace can also be useful, for
example, in calculations of reaction energies, whenthe occupation of orbitals changes in the course ofthe reaction. A suitably chosen V1 subspace should
ensure the rotation of the same set of orbitalsduring the reaction process. Moreover, one canwish that some other virtual orbitals, usually those
with lowest orbital energies, remain unrotated.
(2) V2- subspace—OVOS, active virtual orbitals :
This subspace involves orbitals which representthe OVOS active space. Only V1þV2 virtuals are
considered in the correlation step. So V2 orbitals
must reﬂect the ﬂexibility of the original V2þV3
reduced virtual space.
(3) V3- subspace—virtual orbitals, subjected to the
reduction : Virtual orbitals, contributions from
which are projected to the V2 subspace by the
OVOS procedure. These virtuals are not consideredin the OVOS correlated calculations.

several requirements. They must preserve, as much aspossible, the ﬂexibility of the original virtual space of thesizeV2þV3 after the projection to the reduced OVOS
V2 virtual space. That means they must serve as a good
measure of the change of the correlation energy (or some
other property which could be eventually considered)during the OVOS optimization. Second, the optimiza-tion functional should be simple and must require verylittle computer time. Also, the calculation of the ﬁrstderivative of the functional, needed in the search for theextreme, minimum or maximum, must be simple.Once the optimization functional is deﬁned, we express
it in terms of parameters, which represent the trans-

4. Conclusions
The optimization of the reduced virtual orbital space for
calculations of molecular properties [1, 2] is a promisingmethod for reducing the computer time for demandingcoupled cluster calculations. We have suggested newfunctionals and methods for the optimization of theorbital rotation within the virtual space. Methods areeffective and have been tested for CCSD and CCSD(T)
calculations of reaction energies, spectroscopic con-
stants and the dipole moment of selected molecules.Comparison of results obtained with the full and withthe reduced virtual space has revealed that satisfactoryaccuracy can be preserved with considerable savings ofthe computer time. It is shown that the virtual space forCCSD and the triples electron correlation contributionscan be reduced down to 50% of the original virtual
space, or even more, which means a saving of computer
time by an order of magnitude. We have found thefollowing.
