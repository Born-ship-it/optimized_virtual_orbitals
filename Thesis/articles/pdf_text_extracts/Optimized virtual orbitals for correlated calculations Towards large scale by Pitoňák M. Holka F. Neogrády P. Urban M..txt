Abstract
The optimized virtual orbitals space (OVOS) approach is applied to coupled cluster CCSD( T) calculations of dipole moments and dipole
polarizabilities of selected systems. Particular attention is paid to OVOS reduced to about 50% of the full virtual space, which leads to the
computer time savings larger than one order of magnitude. The ﬂuorine anion, the carbon monoxide, formaldehyde, thiophene, and a push–pull 1-
amino-4-nitrobutadiene molecules serve as testing examples. For all systems are OVOS results compared with the full virtual space CCSD( T)
results. Augmented correlation consistent aug-cc-pVXZ basis sets ( XZD,T, and also Q, 5 in some applications) were used in a systematic way. It
is shown that the performance of OVOS improves with increasing the basis set size. OVOS method is shown to be useful in reliable extrapolations
to the basis set limit. With the standard aug-cc-pVTZ basis set OVOS CCSD( T) dipole moments differ from the full virtual space results typically
by 0.002 a.u. The largest difference is 0.006 a.u. Components of the dipole polarizabilities differ typically by 0.02–0.05 a.u. For the FKanion
larger basis sets are needed. The difference between the OVOS and the full space CCSD( T) polarizability with the daug-cc-pV5Z basis set is
0.32 a.u. (2.0% of the total value); the complete basis set limits differ by 0.07 a.u.

1. Introduction
The idea of accelerating the highly sophisticated quantum
chemistry calculations remains extremely attractive. Densityfunctional theory (DFT) methods dominate the present effortstowards expanding the applicability of quantum chemistrymethods to really large many-electron molecules. Suchmethods can provide many useful information on molecularproperties, but the price is that the controlled accuracy isusually lost. When treating small or medium size moleculesor molecular complexes we may wish obtaining ‘right result fora right reason’, that is, results with high and controlledaccuracy. The methods of choice in this area are the ‘standard’wave function theory (WFT) methods capable of recoveringthe substantial portion of the electron correlation. Since, thesemethods are normally much more computer time consumingthan DFT methods efforts leading to more efﬁcient algorithms
allowing extended applicability of the highly correlatedmethods are very important. We will focus our attention to
the most frequently used method for accurate calculations of
molecular properties, namely the coupled cluster (CC) method[1–4] . We will restrict ourselves to the single determinant
reference CCSD( T) method [5](coupled clusters with the
iterative treatment of the single and double excitation ampli-tudes and the perturbative treatment of the triple excitations[5,6] ). We note, however, that the progress in the development
of multi-reference CC methods and other methods applicable
also to quasi-degenerate systems is really impressive in recent
years [2,4,7–12] . Methods investigated in the present work can
be used with such CC methods and with other methods suitablefor treating the electron correlation problem in a straight-forward way.
One group of techniques, which may lead to savings of the
computer time needed in CC methods is based on the idea ofmodifying the underlying orbital picture. These methods either
exploit the localization of MO’s (molecular orbitals) [13–17] ,
or use other modiﬁcations, for instance approximate or frozennatural orbitals (ANO’s or FNO’s) [18,19] . Another alternative

Usual one-electron reference in CCSD( T) calculation is the

Although it affects the corre-lation energy signiﬁcantly, it usually leads to only minor effecton calculated properties. Analogously, we could delete corre-

Rigorously deﬁned electro n correlation methods (vari-
ational, perturbative, CC) are invariant to canonicaltransformations (unitary transformations) within the subspaceof OO’s and VO’s, respectively. Thus, we can impose furtherconstrains on the VO’s including a possibility of rotating theseorbitals with respect to their importance in the correlated wavefunction. One particular cho ice implemented in this work
utilizes the optimized virtual orbital space (OVOS), which isobtained by imposing the additional requirement on VO’s,namely the maximum overlap of the ﬁrst-order perturbativewave function deﬁned in the ‘reduced’ and the full virtualorbital space, respectively [22,23] . Alternative possibility of
deﬁning OVOS is using the second-order Hylleraas functionalas implemented by Adamowicz, Bartlett and Sadlej in theirpioneering papers on OVOS [24,25] , or one can use FNO’s
[18,19] obtained by diagonalizing the ﬁrst-order density matrix
and reducing the virtual space afterwards.
The principles and the applicability of the OVOS method to
calculations of molecular properties have been presented in ourprevious papers [22,23] . In the ﬁrst paper, we have carried out
demonstrative calculations on various properties like isomeri-zation and dissociation energies, spectroscopic constants anddipole moments. We were able to show [22] that the OVOSmethod can be used successfully as an efﬁcient tool for
obtaining results corresponding to the complete basis setlimit (CBS limit). In a more recent paper [23],w eh a v e
demonstrated the applicability of OVOS using the ﬁrst-orderoverlap to calculations of electric properties of molecules
containing a heavy metal element, which require considering
the relativistic effects along with rigorous treatment of theelectron correlation. We have also shown [23] that the OVOS
technique can be used within uncontracted basis sets, whichallows avoiding the basis set contraction as a tool for reducingthe basis set size in correlated relativistic calculations. Weremind that selection of the method for the basis set contractionis even less straightforward than it is in non-relativistic
calculations.
In this work, we will demonstrate the usefulness of the
OVOS technique in calculations of the electric dipole momentsand dipole polarizabilities of selected small systems, like theF
Kanion, carbon monoxide, formaldehyde and medium size
molecules, namely thiophene and a ‘push–pull’ 1-amino-4-nitrobutadiene compound. Molecular properties obtained withdifferently reduced OVOS will be compared with large-scale
CCSD( T) calculations within the full virtual space. We will
examine the performance of the OVOS technique along withthe study of the effect of the AO (atomic orbitals) basis setquality. Most convenient for this purpose are the cc-pVXZ,aug-cc-pVXZ and the d-aug-cc-pVXZ ( XZD,T,Q,.) series
of Dunning’s et al. style correlation consistent basis sets[26,27] . The systematic structure of these basis sets allows
extrapolations to the CBS limit [28,29] . We will try to show
that such extrapolations lead to virtually identical electric
properties in the full VOS and the reduced space representedby OVOS. Since, analytic gradient techniques within OVOSare not yet available to us (see, however Ref. [19]) electric
properties were calculated by the FFPT (ﬁnite ﬁeld pertur-bation theory) approach. This method is known to be verysensitive to the accuracy of calculated energies. At the sametime electric properties are known as being sensitive to the
basis set size and quality and can thus serve as a tough test for
the reliability of the OVOS method.
2. Theory overview
2.1. Principles of OVOS
Two arbitrary orthogonal sets of MO’s describing the same
system are related via unitary transformation U. Requiring the
CC energy to be invariant with respect to the unitary rotation U,
then blocks which represent mixing of OO’s and VO’s must bezero. Furthermore, we leave the subspace of OO’s untouched.
Let us split full VOS into three subspaces, V1, V2, and V3,
respectively. V1 represents the subspace of ‘inactive’ VO’s andremains untouched in the OVOS procedure. The principal part
within OVOS is the V2 subspace, which should ‘absorb’ the
‘information’ included in the full VOS. Virtual orbitalsbelonging to the V3 subspace are deleted in the subsequentcorrelation calculation. 2.3. Optimization procedure
The OVOS transformation matrix is obtained in an iterative,
SCF-like procedure, which scales roughly as N5. Depending on
the available core memory which should allow storing of the
double-excitation amplitudes the procedure is usually donein-core. Normally the OVOS optimization takes negligible partof the CPU time of the subsequent CCSD( T) calculation.
Our experience from using OVOS indicates, that although
we can reclaim more than 99% of the full-space correlationenergy when reducing OVOS to one half, this accuracy maynot be sufﬁcient for calculations of sensitive properties like
spectroscopic constants, or pol arizability. Fortunately, the
remedy for increasing the accuracy of OVOS is available.We can use a correction suggested by Adamowicz and Bartlett[24], which lies in exploiting the full-space second-order

3. Results
3.1. The ﬂuorine anion
Demanding tests for the performance of the OVOS method
are calculations of polarizabilities of the ‘diffuse’ systems withhigh symmetry. Molecular orbitals of such systems split intodifferent symmetry representations with a smaller dimensionreducing this way the variational space for the optimizationprocedure. Also, one has to be careful in selecting orbitals
entering into the symmetry adapted virtual space reduced by
the OVOS treatment. All symmetry equivalent orbitals (like,e.g. p-, d-, etc. atomic orbitals) belonging to different symmetryrepresentations must be either included in the reduced space orcompletely omitted (if they contribute to OVOS only little).Finally, we note that our standard reduction within OVOSwould be 50% of the full space of virtual orbitals. Due to therequirement of the balanced reduction within each symmetry
representation we reach this target value only approximately,
see also detailed analysis presented in our paper, Ref. [23].

3.2. Carbon monoxide
The dipole moment calculations of the CO molecule using
OVOS were published recently [22]. Accurate experimental
[34] and theoretical data [18,35–38] are available from the
literature. The crucial importance of the electron correlation for
obtaining even correct sign of the CO dipole moment is
notoriously known, see e.g. [39]. Concerning the dipole
moment as presented in Table 3 we just stress that CCSD( T)
calculations with OVOS reduced to 50% reproduce the full
space results perfectly, particularly when basis sets with higher
cardinal number are used. CBS limit resulting from OVOS
differs from that with the full virtual space by 0.002 a.u. with
the aug-cc-pVXZ series. More detailed analysis of the capa-
bility of OVOS in representing basis set effects with the
cc-pVXZ and aug-cc-pVXZ series was presented previously
[22].
In the present paper, we extend previous OVOS data by
more demanding dipole polarizability calculations of the CO
molecule, see Table 4 .
When reducing OVOS to, say 50%, both polarizability
components must be calculated with well balanced accuracy
for obtaining correct values of the polarizability anisotropy and
the average polarizability. This is not automatically guaranteed
since azzandaxxare calculated in different point groups when
using the ﬁnite ﬁeld technique. Two ways of deﬁning the
OVOS reduction were examined. First, the total number of
virtual orbitals was preserved or, second, the percentage of the
optimization functional (i.e. sum of the ldiagonal values
compared to the full VOS) was kept constant within OVOS
(see Ref. [22]) when calculating both polarizability com-
ponents. When the optimization procedure was well
converging, these two approaches were equivalent. Otherwise
the second option should be preferred.
The full space CCSD( T) results for the dipole polarizability
demonstrate typical effect of the standard cc-pVXZ basis set
augmentation by the diffuse functions, although both azzand
axxcomponents are less sensitive to the electron correlation
effects than was the case with the dipole moment [22]. They arealso less sensitive to the basis set extension (when considering
the aug-cc-pVXZ series). As expected, cc-pVXZ basis sets are
not suitable for calculations of this property. At least the
cc-pV5Z is needed to reach results with acceptable accuracy.
Due to larger sensitivity of ato the cardinal number in the
cc-pVXZ series than in the aug-cc-pVXZ series the CBS limit
of the former set is slightly overestimated particularly for the
parallel component. Of course, using the cc-pVXZ basis sets ismeaningless for calculations of the polarizability. In fact, better
polarizability is obtained with the aug-cc-pVDZ basis set than
with much larger cc-pV5Z basis set. The only purpose ofpresenting results with the cc-pVXZ series is to demonstrate
that all basis set trends observed in the full space of virtuals are
perfectly reproduced with OVOS reduced to 50%. With most
basis sets (notable exception is the smallest cc-pVXZ basis set)

4. Conclusions
Results presented in this paper demonstrate the usefulness
of OVOS in calculations of dipole moments and dipolepolarizabilities with the CCSD( T) approach using routine
(d)aug-cc-pVXZ basis sets. We proceed from a few smallsystems for which relatively large basis sets can be used upto thiophene and 1-amino-4-nitrobutadiene (44 correlatedelectrons). This is the largest molecule for which the full
virtual space needed for comparison with OVOS results was
tractable with the aug-cc-pVDZ and aug-cc-pVTZ basis sets.We pay particular attention to OVOS calculations in which thevirtual space was reduced to one half of the full virtual space.Such reduction leads to the computer time savings larger thanone order of magnitude.
With the above mentioned reduction is the accuracy,
particularly for the dipole moment, impressive. With rela-
tively small aug-cc-pVTZ basis set so obtained CCSD( T)
dipole moments differ from the full virtual space resultstypically by 0.002 a.u. For thiophene, for example, this ismuch smaller error than is the basis set effect within the aug-cc-pVXZ ( XZD,T,Q)s e r i e s .F o rt h e m
xcomponent of1-amino-4-nitrobutadiene is deviation slightly larger,
0.006 a.u., i.e. 0.2% of the full virtual space CCSD( T)
value. Components of the dipole polarizabilities differ typi-cally by 0.02–0.05 a.u. For the F
Kanion at least doubly
augmented basis sets are needed. The difference betweenthe OVOS and the full space CCSD( T) polarizability with
the daug-cc-pV5Z basis set is 0.32 a.u. (2.0% of the totalvalue). This is the largest deviation of the OVOS result fromits full virtual space equivalent. However, the complete basisset limits with OVOS and the full virtual space differ by only0.07 a.u. Since, the CCSD( T) electron correlation energies
using OVOS reduced down to 50% of the full virtual spacediffer from the full space value usually by 1.0–0.01 millihartree, the accuracy of the OVOS dipole polariz-abilities calculated using the ﬁnite ﬁeld method depend on thebalanced energy change due to the external ﬁeld. This is achallenging test for the performance of OVOS. Particularattention should be paid to the balanced reduction of the
symmetry equivalent orbitals entering into different symmetry
representations of the virtual space.
Problems with the convergence of the OVOS optimization
procedure [22] may lead to loosing the control of accuracy.
Such problems were encountered in calculations of the polariz-ability of thiophene with the a ug-cc-pVQZ basis set. We
recommend using the two alternative OVOS reductions insuch cases.
For all molecules the OVOS approach reproduces the
pattern of the basis set dependence of the dipole moment andthe dipole polarizability very well. The performance of OVOSimproves with extending the basis set size. For this reasonOVOS can be used for the basis set extrapolations to thecomplete basis set limit.
While conventional CCSD( T) calculations of molecular
properties are tractable with about 500–600 virtuals (dependingon the number of correlated electrons and the molecularsymmetry) with computationa l resources available to us,
OVOS allows substantional extension of CCSD( T) calcu-
lations. OVOS can be useful either in extending the size ofthe basis set for calculations of relatively small moleculesallowing this way to approach closer to the complete basis setlimit or it can be used for calculations of larger molecules,which cannot be treated by conventional CCSD( T) methods.
The present research also opens a route to more efﬁcientcalculations of higher excitations than those considered inCCSD( T).
Present results extend the applicability of the OVOS
techniques investigated previously in calculations of molecularspectroscopic constants, dissociation and reaction energies[22], and in quasi-relativistic CCSD( T) calculations of mol-
ecular properties of a heavy metal element containing smallmolecules [23].
